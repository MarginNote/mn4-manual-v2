> 
> The vast number of cards in MarginNote is a precious asset of your knowledge. But true wisdom lies not only in accumulation, but in whether you can call up and combine these knowledge fragments whenever needed, forming a powerful tool for solving a specific problem.
> 
> Beyond helping you filter and manage cards (see: [Card Grouping Board ①: Precisely Filter from a Vast Collection of Cards](../dJvh1K4GEKaJYtZ14zbfgj/index.md)), the `card grouping board`'s core purpose is to let you easily "build a personal knowledge collection": for example, aggregating all your mistake cards into a mistake collection, or gathering inspiration cards into an inspiration library... turning your knowledge from a static warehouse into "living water" that can be freely broken apart and recombined, always ready to serve your most important learning goal of the moment.

## **From a "Universal Card Library" to a "Dedicated Collection Library"**

The Zettelkasten-styled card box is a "universal library," whereas each brand-new mind map you **generate with one tap** from your filter results is a "dedicated collection library." As the saying goes, "a thousand days making cards, one moment using them": extract the relevant content from the universal library to create a new collection that is highly focused and pure in content, helping you tackle any important exam, project, or presentation.

- How to do it: In the `card grouping board`, after filtering out the cards you need, select them and drag them onto a blank area of the mind map to create new cards in the form of references.
- Example use cases:
  - **Pre-exam mistake compilation**: Filter all cards whose tags contain #mistakes or that have a specific color to generate a dedicated review handbook that goes straight to your weak points.
  - **Quick reference for core concepts**: Search all cards that contain definitions to rapidly build a knowledge framework before the exam and consolidate your memory.
  - Compiling exam points from past papers: Filter and reorganize cards on similar exam points from past-paper PDFs across different years to easily uncover patterns in how questions are set.
  - **Curated picks for a book club**: Before preparing a book-sharing session, there's no need to leaf through the whole book again—simply pull up all the highlight cards you made for this book, and the thread of your presentation is clear at a glance.
  - **Literature synthesis report**: Create a tag for your paper's core argument (e.g., "the ethical challenges of artificial intelligence"), then filter out all related cases, data, and citations with one tap.
  - **A treasure chest of inspiration and material**: Tag every fragment of inspiration from your daily reading that strikes you as "surprising" or "useful" with a single unified tag, and when you hit a creative block, open this collection library to spark new ideas.
  - **A reference set for project planning**: When kicking off a new project, filter out all your past cards on "project management methods," "competitive analysis," and "design templates" to quickly set up your working framework.

> 💡**The cornerstone of all these powerful knowledge collections is a small, everyday effort**: **labeling your cards well**
> 
> Whether it's a precise tag, a specific color, or a set of essential keywords—it is exactly these seemingly trivial labeling habits that form the "navigation map" for precise filtering and knowledge reorganization down the road.
> 
> We recommend taking a few seconds the moment you create a card to classify it, and doing so consistently to establish your own conventions. For example, use "red" to mark questionable points, "blue" to mark cases, and the "#CoreConcept" tag to mark key theorems.

## **Dynamically Update Your Collection Library, Growing in Sync with the Source**

The "personal collection" you create is **dynamic and alive**. When you make new cards that meet your criteria (such as new mistakes or new paper material), simply run the same filter conditions again to **update the collection library with one tap**, ensuring it always stays in sync with your latest body of knowledge.
